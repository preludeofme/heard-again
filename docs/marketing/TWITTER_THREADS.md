## Thread 2: "The Right Way to Use AI for Preserving a Loved One's Voice"

**Angle:** Ethics of Voice Cloning & Consent

**1/6**
AI voice cloning is one of the most powerful — and dangerous — technologies of our time. Used carelessly, it's a privacy violation and a deepfake weapon. Used responsibly, it can help families preserve a loved one's voice with dignity. Here's the ethical line.

**2/6**
The hard rule: **consent must come first, always.** You never clone someone's voice without their explicit, informed permission. Not as a surprise. Not as a "gift." Not posthumously unless they left clear documented consent while alive. This is non-negotiable.

**3/6**
The second rule: **the person dictates the terms.** How is their voice used? Where is it stored? Who has access? Can it be deleted? These aren't technical questions — they're human ones. The tool should empower the person, not exploit them.

**4/6**
The third rule: **transparency is mandatory.** Any AI-generated voice content must be clearly labeled. Listeners deserve to know when they're hearing a real recording versus a synthetic recreation. Deception destroys trust and undermines the entire purpose of preservation.

**5/6**
Heard Again was built on these principles from day one. Consent-first architecture. No voice data leaves your control unless you choose. AI personas are grounded only in documents the family has deliberately shared. Every voice profile requires documented permission.

**6/6**
Voice cloning should preserve memory, not manufacture reality. When done with consent, transparency, and respect, it's one of the most beautiful uses of AI there is. Done right, your grandmother's voice lives on because she chose to share it. → heardagain.com

## Thread 3: "How to Digitize Your Family's Audio in 2026"

**Angle:** Practical Tips / Heard Again as a solution

**1/5**
You've got shoeboxes full of cassette tapes, a pile of old SD cards, and voicemails from people you miss. 2026 is the year to digitize it all before the media degrades. Here's your practical five-step playbook.

**2/5**
**Step 1: Gather & Prioritize.** Collect everything — tapes, MiniDiscs, digital files, voicemails. Prioritize magnetic media first (cassettes, VHS, answering machine microcassettes) — they degrade fastest. Digital files can wait, but don't let them sit on dying hard drives.

**3/5**
**Step 2: Capture with quality gear.** Don't use a microphone pointed at a speaker — that introduces room noise and compression artifacts. Get a proper USB cassette capture deck ($30-60 used) or a Resolve audio interface. Record at 24-bit/48kHz minimum. WAV or FLAC, never MP3 for archival.

**4/5**
**Step 3: Organize before you upload.** Name files with who, what, when: `Grandma_Maria_2020_BirthdayMessage.wav`. Add metadata tags. Create a simple spreadsheet tracking what exists and what's missing. Future you will thank past you — I promise.

**5/5**
**Step 4: Store somewhere you control.** This is where Heard Again comes in. Upload your digitized audio to a platform built for family preservation — not data extraction. Automatically transcribed, searchable, shared with the people you choose, on infrastructure you can self-host. Your memories, your rules. → heardagain.com
